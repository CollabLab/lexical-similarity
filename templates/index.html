LexSim: A Tool for Visualizing Lexical Similarity in Text-Based Communication

LexSim is a research tool that enables researchers to study lexical similarity as it unfolds over the course of a conversation. It can currently be used on single two-person text-based conversations. In the future we aim to include options to explore more general trends in aggregate conversational dynamics, and examine differences in how similarity tracks over time across sets of groups a research may wish to compare (e.g., high trust vs. low trust groups, *need better examples here*).

If you use the tool in your work, or are interested in further details in how it works, please cite or see the following publication:
Liebman, N., & Gergle, D. (2016). Capturing Turn-by-turn Lexical Similarity in Text-based Communication. Proceedings of the ACM Conference on Computer-Supported Cooperative Work and Social Computing (CSCW 2016), New York: ACM Press.

 What is Lexical Similarity

Speakers often come to use similar words during their conversation. This is known as lexical entrainment, and this similarity in word use has been shown to be associated with how much people like one another (Chartrand & Bargh, 1999) as well as increased feelings of attractiveness (Nass & Lee, 2000). Benefits have also been shown in technologically-mediated settings such as self-reported quality of an interaction Niederhoffer & Pennebaker, 2002), trust (Scissors, Gill & Gergle, 2008), and group cohesiveness (Gonzales, Hancock & Pennebaker, 2010).

CollabLab

The CollabLab is a laboratory at Northwestern University. Our current research programs span a number of areas from understanding how various forms of visual information influence our social interactions to developing dynamic visualizations of interaction patterns in online environments to building computational models to account for real-time contextual information and support interaction.

Darren Gergle (Ph.D., Carnegie Mellon University) is the director of the CollabLab and is a faculty member in Communication Studies and Electrical Engineering & Computer Science (by courtesy) at Northwestern University.
